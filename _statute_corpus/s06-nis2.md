# Statute Digest: NIS2 Directive - Directive (EU) 2022/2555

## Citation
Directive (EU) 2022/2555 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 14 December 2022 on measures for a high common level of cybersecurity across the Union, amending Regulation (EU) No 910/2014 and Directive (EU) 2018/1972, and repealing Directive (EU) 2016/1148 (NIS2 Directive).

## Authority Tier
EU Directive (Binding as to the result to be achieved, but requires national transposition into the laws of each Member State).

## Currency
*   **Entered into force:** 16 January 2023.
*   **Transposition deadline:** 17 October 2024.
*   **Date of application:** Generally from 18 October 2024.

## Scope
The NIS2 Directive sets a high common level of cybersecurity across the EU. It applies to:
*   **Essential Entities (EE):** Large enterprises in sectors of high criticality (energy, transport, health, water, digital infrastructure, etc.).
*   **Important Entities (IE):** Medium-sized and large enterprises in other critical sectors (postal services, waste management, chemicals, food, manufacturing, etc.).
*   **Small/Micro Entities:** Generally excluded unless they play a critical role (e.g., DNS providers, trust service providers).

**IMPORTANT FOR ADVOCATES:** As this is a Directive, always check the specific national transposition law of the relevant Member State for exact wording and potentially stricter requirements.

## Section-by-section digest

### Article 2: Scope
*   **Plain-English summary:** The directive applies to all medium and large companies in the listed sectors. Size is determined by having 50+ employees or €10M+ annual turnover. However, some critical entities are covered regardless of their size (like telecom providers or public administration).
*   **Statutory text (Summary):**
    1. Applies to public or private entities of a type referred to in Annex I (High Criticality) or II (Other Critical) that are medium-sized or larger.
    2. Regardless of size, applies to: providers of public electronic communications, trust service providers, TLD name registries, DNS service providers, and entities identified as critical under the CER Directive.
*   **Citation format:** Art. 2 Dir. (EU) 2022/2555.
*   **Cross-references:** Annex I & II (Sector lists), SME Recommendation 2003/361/EC (Size definitions).
*   **Practical use for solo advocate:** Determining whether a client or an opposing entity is legally mandated to meet these cybersecurity standards.
*   **STATUS: VERIFIED**

### Article 20: Governance
*   **Plain-English summary:** Management bodies (e.g., Boards of Directors) are directly responsible for cybersecurity. They must approve the company's security measures, supervise their implementation, and can be held personally liable for failures. Executives are also required to undergo cybersecurity training.
*   **Statutory text (Summary):**
    1. Member States shall ensure management bodies approve cybersecurity risk-management measures, oversee implementation, and are liable for infringements.
    2. Members of management bodies shall follow regular cybersecurity training.
*   **Citation format:** Art. 20 Dir. (EU) 2022/2555.
*   **Cross-references:** Article 21 (Risk-management measures), Article 32 (Supervisory powers).
*   **Practical use for solo advocate:** Assigning liability in litigation or during a breach. Proving that the "C-suite" failed in their oversight duties.
*   **STATUS: VERIFIED**

### Article 21: Cybersecurity risk-management measures
*   **Plain-English summary:** Entities must implement "appropriate and proportionate" security measures. This includes at least ten specific areas: risk analysis, incident handling, business continuity (backups), supply chain security, encryption, and multi-factor authentication (MFA).
*   **Statutory text (Summary):**
    1. Entities shall take technical, operational and organisational measures to manage risks to the security of network and information systems.
    2. Minimum measures include: (a) policies on risk analysis; (b) incident handling; (c) business continuity; (d) supply chain security; (e) security in acquisition/development; (f) policies to assess effectiveness; (g) cyber hygiene; (h) cryptography/encryption; (i) HR security/access control; (j) MFA or continuous authentication.
*   **Citation format:** Art. 21 Dir. (EU) 2022/2555.
*   **Cross-references:** Article 23 (Reporting), Article 24 (Supply chain security).
*   **Practical use for solo advocate:** Establishing the "standard of care" in a negligence or breach of contract claim following a cyberattack.
*   **STATUS: VERIFIED**

### Article 23: Reporting obligations
*   **Plain-English summary:** If a "significant" incident occurs, the entity must notify the authorities in stages: an "early warning" within 24 hours, a full notification within 72 hours, and a final report within one month.
*   **Statutory text (Summary):**
    1. Entities shall notify the CSIRT or competent authority of any significant incident.
    2. Timeline: (a) Early warning within 24 hours of awareness; (b) Incident notification within 72 hours; (c) Final report within one month of the notification.
    3. An incident is significant if it causes severe operational disruption or financial loss, or affects others by causing considerable damage.
*   **Citation format:** Art. 23 Dir. (EU) 2022/2555.
*   **Cross-references:** Article 21 (Risk-management).
*   **Practical use for solo advocate:** Checking compliance for a client after an incident, or using a failure to report as evidence of non-compliance in a regulatory or legal action.
*   **STATUS: VERIFIED**
