## Structural Skeleton
1. **Title of the Act**
   - Type (Regulation/Directive/Decision)
   - Number and Year
   - Subject matter
2. **Preamble**
   - Citations (Legal basis: "Having regard to...")
   - Recitals (Justification: "Whereas...")
3. **Enacting Terms**
   - Subject matter and scope
   - Definitions
   - Substantive provisions (Rights/Obligations)
   - Procedural/Administrative provisions
   - Final provisions (Entry into force, addressees)
4. **Annexes** (Technical details, forms)

## Drafting Conventions
- **Language Policy**: 24 official languages are equally authoritative. Drafters must avoid language-specific idioms.
- **Voice**: Use the imperative for binding rules ("shall" or present tense depending on language; in English, "shall" is standard for obligations).
- **Recitals**: Must be numbered. They must not contain normative rules or technical details.
- **Consistency**: The same term must be used for the same concept throughout the document.
- **Gender-Neutral Language**: Use inclusive language where possible.
- **Numbering**: Articles use Arabic numerals. Paragraphs (sub-articles) use Arabic numerals in parentheses. Items use lowercase letters in parentheses.
- **Citations**: 
  - Treaties: e.g., "Article 114 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union (TFEU)".
  - Secondary Law: "Regulation (EU) 2016/679 of the European Parliament and of the Council...".
  - Official Journal: "OJ L 119, 4.5.2016, p. 1".

## Fair-Use Excerpt (if applicable)
"The preamble shall consist of citations and recitals. The citations shall set out the legal basis of the act... The recitals shall set out concise reasons for the chief provisions of the enacting terms, without reproducing or paraphrasing them. They shall not contain normative provisions." (Joint Practical Guide, Guidelines 10 & 11).

## Practical Use for Solo Advocate / Small Firm
Critical for drafting submissions to the CJEU, regulatory compliance filings, or cross-border EU-level contracts. Ensures that terminology matches the "Eurospeak" used by regulators and courts, reducing the risk of misinterpretation.
