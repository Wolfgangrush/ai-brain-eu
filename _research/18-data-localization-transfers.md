## Currency Warning
The **EU-US Data Privacy Framework** (2023) is the current legal basis for transfers to the US. However, always check for "Schrems III" style challenges in the news.

## Key Facts
1. **Data Localization:** The EU generally forbids national laws from requiring "data residency" within a single state (Free Flow of Non-Personal Data Regulation). However, GDPR personal data must stay within the EEA unless a transfer mechanism is used.
2. **Adequacy Decisions:** Data can flow freely to countries deemed "adequate" (e.g., UK, Japan, Canada, South Korea).
3. **EU-US Transfers:** Lawyers can use US-based cloud services (Microsoft 365, Google Workspace) if the provider is certified under the **EU-US Data Privacy Framework**.
4. **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs):** If a country is not "adequate," lawyers must sign SCCs with the data recipient.
5. **Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA):** Even with SCCs, lawyers must assess if the law of the third country (e.g., India, China) undermines the protection.
6. **Legal Privilege Exception:** Art 49 GDPR allows transfers if "necessary for the establishment, exercise or defence of legal claims," which can sometimes be used by advocates for specific case-related data.
7. **Sovereign Clouds:** Many EU lawyers are moving to "European" cloud providers (e.g., OVHcloud, T-Systems) to avoid the complexity of international transfers.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"Any transfer of personal data ... shall take place only if ... the conditions laid down in this Chapter are complied with." - Article 44, GDPR.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
- **Vendor Check:** Ensure your practice management software servers are located in the EEA or a country with an Adequacy Decision.
- **Client Disclosure:** Always inform clients in the Privacy Notice if their data might be transferred outside the EU (e.g., if you use a US-based email service).
- **Safe Harboring:** For highly sensitive litigation data, prefer local, encrypted storage over global cloud providers.
