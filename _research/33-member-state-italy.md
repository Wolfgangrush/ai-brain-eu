## Key Facts
1. **Civil Law System:** Based on the 1942 Civil Code (*Codice Civile*), which integrated commercial and civil law.
2. **Ordinary Courts Hierarchy:** Giudice di Pace (minor), Tribunale (general), Corte d'Appello (appeal), and Corte Suprema di Cassazione (Supreme Court).
3. **Administrative Justice:** Handled by the *Tribunale Amministrativo Regionale* (TAR) and the *Consiglio di Stato*.
4. **Constitutional Court:** *Corte Costituzionale* sits in Rome and rules on the constitutionality of laws.
5. **CSM:** The *Consiglio Superiore della Magistratura* ensures the independence of judges and prosecutors from the executive.
6. **Public Prosecutor:** Prosecutors (*Pubblico Ministero*) are members of the judiciary and have a high degree of independence.
7. **Criminal Procedure:** Moved toward an accusatorial system in 1989, but retains some inquisitorial elements.
8. **Avvocato:** To become a lawyer, one must pass a state exam after a period of traineeship (*pratica forense*).
9. **Cassa Forense:** The mandatory social security and pension fund for Italian lawyers.
10. **Digital Trial (*Processo Civile Telematico*):** Italy has made significant strides in mandatory e-filing for civil cases.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The judiciary is an order that is autonomous and independent of any other power." — Article 104, Italian Constitution.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Solo advocates in Italy must master the *Processo Civile Telematico* (PCT) for all civil filings. They should also be aware of the long duration of Italian proceedings, making alternative dispute resolution and interim measures critical components of their practice.
