## Currency Warning
The Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) underwent a major structural reform effective **October 1, 2024**. This reform transferred jurisdiction for preliminary rulings in specific technical areas to the General Court. Ensure procedural guides reflect this 2024 redistribution.

## Key Facts
1. **The Institution:** The Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) is the judicial authority of the EU, ensuring uniform interpretation and application of EU law. It is headquartered in Luxembourg.
2. **Two-Tier Structure:** The CJEU consists of two main courts: the **Court of Justice** (often called ECJ) and the **General Court**.
3. **Court of Justice (ECJ):**
   - **Composition:** 27 Judges (one per Member State) and 11 Advocates General.
   - **Mandate:** Highest court of the EU. Handles preliminary rulings (referred by national courts) on general EU law, actions for failure to fulfill obligations (infringement), and appeals from the General Court.
4. **General Court:**
   - **Composition:** 54 Judges (two per Member State).
   - **Mandate:** Handles direct actions brought by individuals, companies, and some Member State actions against EU institutions (e.g., competition law, state aid, trade marks).
   - **New Power (2024):** Since Oct 2024, it has jurisdiction to give preliminary rulings in six technical areas: VAT, excise duties, Customs Code, tariff classification, passenger compensation, and greenhouse gas emission trading.
5. **Advocates General:** Their role is to provide independent, impartial "opinions" to the Court on cases requiring a new point of law. While not binding, these opinions are highly influential.
6. **Language of the Case:** In preliminary rulings, the language is that of the referring national court. In direct actions, the applicant chooses one of the 24 official EU languages.
7. **Binding Nature:** Decisions of the CJEU are final and binding on all national courts within the EU. There is no appeal from the ECJ.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The Court of Justice of the European Union shall include the Court of Justice, the General Court and specialised courts. It shall ensure that in the interpretation and application of the Treaties the law is observed." — *Article 19(1), Treaty on European Union (TEU)*.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
- **Preliminary Rulings:** If a solo advocate represents a client in a national court where a point of EU law is unclear, they can request the national judge to refer the question to the CJEU. This is the primary "gateway" for solo advocates to access the CJEU.
- **Direct Actions:** A solo advocate representing a business against a Commission decision (e.g., a fine or a rejected trade mark) would file the action directly in the **General Court**.
- **Case Law Research:** Advocates must monitor CJEU judgments as they override national law in areas of EU competence.
