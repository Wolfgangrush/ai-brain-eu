## Currency Warning
The "Schrems II" judgment (C-311/18) invalidated the Privacy Shield for US transfers. The new **EU-U.S. Data Privacy Framework** (2023) currently provides an adequacy decision for participating US companies, but it is subject to ongoing legal challenges.

## Key Facts
1. **General Principle:** Personal data can be transferred outside the EEA only if the destination country ensures an "adequate level of protection" (Art. 44).
2. **Adequacy Decisions (Art. 45):** The Commission can formally decide that a third country (e.g., UK, Japan, Canada, South Korea) provides adequate protection. In such cases, no further safeguards are needed.
3. **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs):** In the absence of an adequacy decision, exporters can use Commission-approved SCCs to provide "appropriate safeguards" (Art. 46).
4. **Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs):** For multinational groups, a set of internal rules for transfers within the group, approved by a DPA (Art. 47).
5. **Derogations (Art. 49):** Limited exceptions for specific situations (e.g., explicit consent, necessity for the performance of a contract, or legal claims).
6. **Transfer Impact Assessments (TIA):** Following Schrems II, data exporters must assess the law of the destination country to ensure that the safeguards (like SCCs) are actually effective against local surveillance laws.
7. **Supplementary Measures:** If the destination's laws are too intrusive, the exporter must apply technical measures (e.g., strong end-to-end encryption where the provider has no keys).
8. **EDPB Guidelines:** The European Data Protection Board (EDPB) provides binding guidance on how to conduct TIAs and what measures are sufficient.
9. **Role of DPAs:** National authorities can suspend data flows if they find that the destination country does not offer an equivalent level of protection.
10. **Data Residency:** Many firms choose "data residency" (keeping data strictly within the EEA) to avoid the complexity and risk of Chapter V compliance.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"Any transfer of personal data... shall take place only if... the conditions laid down in this Chapter are complied with by the controller and processor." — Article 44.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
If a solo advocate uses a US-based cloud service (e.g., Google Workspace, Microsoft 365), they must ensure the provider is certified under the **EU-U.S. Data Privacy Framework**. If not, they must have a signed DPA with SCCs and have conducted a TIA. Failing to do so is a major GDPR risk and a potential breach of professional ethics regarding client data protection.
