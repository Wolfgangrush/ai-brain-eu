Statute (Regulation)

## Currency Warning
Lawyers' firm structures are primarily regulated by national Bar rules. These EU-level structures are "add-ons" for cross-border collaboration.

## Key Facts
1. **European Economic Interest Grouping (EEIG):** A flexible legal entity designed to facilitate cross-border cooperation between firms or individuals in different Member States.
2. **EEIG Purpose:** To improve or increase the business of its members; it is not meant to make profits for itself, but for its members.
3. **EEIG Liability:** Members of an EEIG have unlimited joint and several liability for its debts (Art. 24).
4. **Societas Europaea (SE):** A European Public Limited Liability Company. It allows companies from different Member States to merge or form a holding company.
5. **SE Registration:** An SE is registered in the Member State where it has its registered office. It can easily transfer its office to another Member State.
6. **Establishment of Lawyers:** Directive 98/5/EC allows lawyers to practice permanently in another Member State under their home-country title.
7. **Integration:** After three years of "effective and regular activity," a lawyer practicing under their home title can apply for full admission to the host Bar.
8. **Lawyer Companies:** Some Member States allow lawyers to practice as limited liability companies (LLCs), while others (like some German states in the past) had stricter requirements for partnerships.
9. **Cross-Border Partnerships:** Lawyers from different Member States can form partnerships, but they must comply with the rules of the host Member State regarding the sharing of fees and name usage.
10. **Professional Indemnity:** Regardless of the structure, EU law (Directive 2006/123/EC) and Bar rules require lawyers to have adequate professional liability insurance.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The purpose of a grouping shall be to facilitate or develop the economic activities of its members and to improve or increase the results of those activities." — Article 3(1), EEIG Regulation.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
A solo advocate wishing to partner with a small firm in another country (e.g., a Dutch and a Belgian firm) can form an **EEIG**. This allows them to have a shared "European" identity and office without the complexity of a full merger or the tax issues of a cross-border subsidiary.
