## Key Facts
1. **Civil Law System:** Rooted in the 1889 Civil Code (*Código Civil*) and the 1978 Constitution.
2. **Five Jurisdictional Orders:** Civil, Criminal, Contentious-Administrative, Social (Labor), and Military.
3. **Court Hierarchy:** Juzgados de Primera Instancia (Local), Audiencias Provinciales (Provincial), Tribunales Superiores de Justicia (Regional), and Tribunal Supremo (National).
4. **Audiencia Nacional:** A specialized national court for terrorism, organized crime, and major financial fraud.
5. **Constitutional Court:** *Tribunal Constitucional* handles the *Recurso de Amparo* for protection of fundamental rights.
6. **Procurador:** A unique legal professional who represents the client procedurally, while the *Abogado* provides the legal defense.
7. **CGPJ:** The *Consejo General del Poder Judicial* is the governing body of the judiciary.
8. **LexNET:** The mandatory secure telecommunications system for the exchange of information between judicial bodies and legal practitioners.
9. **Autonomous Communities:** Regional High Courts (TSJ) apply both national and regional law (where specific *Foral* law exists).
10. **State Exams:** Entry to the bar requires a Master’s degree in Access to the Legal Profession and a national entrance exam.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"Justice emanates from the people and is administered on behalf of the King by Judges and Magistrates members of the Judicial Power." — Article 117, Spanish Constitution.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
A solo advocate (*Abogado*) must work closely with a *Procurador* in most court proceedings. They must also be proficient in **LexNET**, as it is the sole channel for receiving court notifications and filing pleadings.
