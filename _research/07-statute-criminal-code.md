# Statute: Criminal Law Harmonization — European Union

## Authority Tier
Statute (Directive)

## Currency Warning
EU criminal law is harmonized through Directives that Member States must transpose. Definitions and minimum penalties may vary slightly across jurisdictions until fully harmonized.

## Key Facts
1. **Terrorism (Directive 2017/541):** Harmonizes the definition of terrorist offenses, including traveling for terrorism, providing training, and financing terrorism.
2. **Human Trafficking (Directive 2011/36):** Establishes minimum rules concerning the definition of criminal offenses and sanctions in the area of trafficking in human beings.
3. **Cybercrime (Directive 2013/40):** Harmonizes definitions of offenses like illegal access to information systems and illegal data interference.
4. **Child Sexual Abuse (Directive 2011/93):** Sets minimum rules for the definition of criminal offenses and sanctions related to child sexual exploitation.
5. **Money Laundering (Directive 2018/1673):** Harmonizes the definition of money laundering offenses and sanctions (different from the AML administrative package in File 27).
6. **Corruption:** EU law targets both active and passive corruption involving EU officials and corruption in the private sector (Framework Decision 2003/568/JHA).
7. **Market Abuse (Directive 2014/57):** Establishes criminal sanctions for insider dealing and market manipulation.
8. **Environmental Crime (Directive 2008/99):** Requires Member States to provide for criminal penalties for serious infringements of EU environmental law.
9. **Minimum Sanctions:** Directives often specify "effective, proportionate and dissuasive" criminal penalties, including minimum terms of imprisonment for the most serious offenses.
10. **Victims' Rights (Directive 2012/29):** Establishes minimum standards on the rights, support, and protection of victims of crime across the EU.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The Union may establish minimum rules concerning the definition of criminal offences and sanctions in the areas of particularly serious crime with a cross-border dimension." — Article 83(1) TFEU.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Criminal defense lawyers must be aware of the "EU dimension" of the charges their clients face. If a national law is stricter than the harmonized EU Directive or violates the procedural rights guaranteed by EU law (e.g., right to information), the advocate can challenge the legality of the national measure.
