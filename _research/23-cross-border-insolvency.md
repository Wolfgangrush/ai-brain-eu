## Authority Tier
Statute (Regulation (EU) 2015/848)

## Currency Warning
Note the "anti-forum shopping" look-back periods (3 months for companies, 6 months for individuals) when determining jurisdiction based on COMI.

## Key Facts
1. **COMI (Centre of Main Interests):** Jurisdiction for main insolvency proceedings is where the debtor's COMI is located (presumed to be the registered office).
2. **Automatic Recognition:** A judgment opening insolvency in one Member State is automatically recognized in all others.
3. **Universal Scope:** Main proceedings cover all the debtor's assets across the EU.
4. **Secondary Proceedings:** Can be opened in a state where the debtor has an "establishment." These are restricted to local assets.
5. **Insolvency Registers:** Member States must maintain public electronic insolvency registers, which are interconnected via the e-Justice portal.
6. **Group Insolvencies:** Provides a framework for coordinating the insolvency of members of a corporate group (Group Coordination Proceedings).
7. **Synthetic Proceedings:** Allows the practitioner in the main state to give an "undertaking" to local creditors in another state to avoid the need for costly secondary proceedings.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The courts of the Member State within the territory of which the centre of the debtor's main interests is situated shall have jurisdiction to open insolvency proceedings." - Article 3(1), Regulation 2015/848.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
- **Asset Tracing:** Use the "Interconnected Insolvency Registers" on the e-Justice portal to check if a debtor is bankrupt in any other EU state.
- **Protecting Creditors:** If a client's debtor goes bust in another EU state, the advocate must lodge a claim (Form G) with the foreign practitioner within the specified time limits.
- **COMI Challenges:** If a debtor moves their registered office just before filing for bankruptcy, the advocate can challenge the jurisdiction using the "look-back" rules.
