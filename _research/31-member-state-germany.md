## Key Facts
1. **Civil Law System:** Germany has a civil law system based on codified statutes (e.g., BGB for civil law, StGB for criminal law).
2. **Five Branches of Judiciary:** Specialized branches for Ordinary (Civil/Criminal), Administrative, Labor, Social, and Fiscal matters.
3. **Ordinary Courts Hierarchy:** Amtsgericht (Local), Landgericht (Regional), Oberlandesgericht (Higher Regional), and Bundesgerichtshof (Federal Court of Justice).
4. **Constitutional Court:** The *Bundesverfassungsgericht* (BVerfG) sits outside the ordinary hierarchy and rules on constitutional matters.
5. **No Jury:** Cases are decided by professional judges, sometimes assisted by lay judges (*Schöffen*) in criminal and specialized courts.
6. **Mandatory Representation:** Lawyers are mandatory in all courts from the *Landgericht* level upwards (*Anwaltszwang*).
7. **BGH Lawyers:** Only a small, specialized group of lawyers is admitted to represent clients in civil cases before the Federal Court of Justice.
8. **Rechtspfleger:** Specialized judicial officers who handle many non-contentious matters (e.g., land registry, company registry).
9. **Legal Education:** Requires two state exams and a two-year clerkship (*Referendariat*).
10. **Federalism:** Most courts are state-run (*Länder*), but they apply uniform federal law.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"Judicial power shall be vested in the judges; it shall be exercised by the Federal Constitutional Court, by the federal courts provided for in this Basic Law, and by the courts of the Länder." — Article 92 Basic Law (*Grundgesetz*).

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
A solo advocate in Germany must be aware of the strict subject-matter jurisdiction. Filing a labor dispute in an ordinary civil court will lead to a dismissal or transfer. The *Anwaltszwang* ensures that solo practitioners have a steady stream of work in higher-level disputes.
