## Currency Warning
Product Liability rules are in a transition period. The new Directive (EU) 2024/2853 was adopted in late 2024 and must be transposed by Member States by **December 9, 2026**.

## Key Facts
1. **National Competence:** Generally, limitation periods are governed by the national law of the Member State. EU law only sets minimum standards for specific types of claims.
2. **Product Liability (Directive 85/374/EEC):**
   - **3 years:** For the recovery of damages. Begins when the claimant became aware (or should have been aware) of the damage, defect, and producer's identity.
   - **10 years (Extinction):** A "long-stop" period. Rights are extinguished 10 years after the product was put into circulation.
3. **New Product Liability (Directive 2024/2853):**
   - Retains the 3/10 year rule.
   - **25 years:** Extension of the long-stop period for personal injuries that are slow-onset (e.g., latent diseases from chemicals).
4. **Competition Law Damages (Directive 2014/104/EU):**
   - **Minimum 5 years:** Member States must provide at least 5 years to bring a claim.
   - **Trigger:** Does not start until the infringement has ceased AND the claimant knows of the harm and infringer's identity.
   - **Suspension:** The clock is suspended during investigations by the European Commission or national competition authorities.
5. **Effective Protection:** The CJEU (e.g., in Case C-606/21 *Heureka*) has ruled that national limitation rules must not make it "practically impossible or excessively difficult" to exercise rights conferred by EU law.
6. **State Liability (Francovich):** Claims against a Member State for failing to implement EU law are also subject to national limitation periods, provided they are not less favorable than domestic claims (Equivalence).

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"Member States shall ensure that the limitation period for bringing an action for damages is at least five years." - Article 10(3), Directive 2014/104/EU.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
- **Early Assessment:** Always check the *dies a quo* (start date). In EU law matters, the clock often doesn't start until the "knowledge" requirement is met.
- **Investigation Suspension:** If representing a client in a cartel case, check if an ongoing Commission investigation has suspended the limitation period.
- **National Variance:** While the Directive says "at least 5 years," some states (like Ireland or Germany) may have different implementation details. Always cross-reference with national code.
