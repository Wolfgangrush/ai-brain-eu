# Statute: Evidence and Investigation (EIO) — European Union

## Source URL
https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2014/41/oj

## Last Verified
2026-05-18

## Authority Tier
Statute (Directive)

## Currency Warning
The European Investigation Order (EIO) Directive replaced most of the earlier rules on mutual legal assistance in criminal matters between Member States.

## Key Facts
1. **European Investigation Order (EIO):** A judicial decision issued or validated by a judicial authority of a Member State to have one or several specific investigative measures carried out in another Member State to obtain evidence.
2. **Mutual Recognition:** The EIO is based on the principle of mutual recognition; the executing authority must carry out the request as if it were a domestic investigation.
3. **Scope:** Covers all investigative measures (e.g., hearing witnesses, searches and seizures, interception of communications, monitoring bank transactions).
4. **Standard Form:** The EIO must be issued using a standard multilingual form (Annex A) to ensure clarity and speed.
5. **Deadlines:** The executing State must decide on recognition within 30 days and carry out the measure within 90 days.
6. **Grounds for Refusal:** Very limited (e.g., immunity, national security interests, or if the measure would violate the *ne bis in idem* principle).
7. **Electronic Evidence (e-Evidence):** New regulations (2023) allow judicial authorities to order service providers (like big tech) in other Member States to produce or preserve electronic data (e.g., emails, cloud data) directly.
8. **Right to a Remedy:** Suspects and persons affected by an EIO have the right to legal remedies equivalent to those available in a domestic case.
9. **Costs:** Generally, the executing State bears the costs of the investigative measures.
10. **Validation:** If an EIO is issued by a non-judicial authority (e.g., police), it must be validated by a judge, court, or prosecutor before being sent abroad.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The EIO should be based on the principle of mutual recognition... it should be carried out with the same celerity and priority as for a similar domestic case." — Recital 11.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
An advocate for a suspect can request that the investigating judge issue an EIO to obtain exculpatory evidence from another Member State (e.g., travel records or witness testimony). Conversely, if the prosecution uses evidence obtained via an EIO, the advocate should check if the procedural safeguards of both the issuing and executing States were respected.
