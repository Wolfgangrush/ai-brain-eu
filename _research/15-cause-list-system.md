## Key Facts
1. **Judicial Calendar (CURIA):** The Court of Justice and General Court publish their judicial calendar online, listing all upcoming hearings, delivery of Opinions, and delivery of Judgments.
2. **Searchable Database:** Hearings can be searched by case number, date, or court (ECJ or General Court).
3. **Public Access:** Hearings at the CJEU are generally public unless the Court decides otherwise for specific reasons (e.g., protection of sensitive data).
4. **Live Streaming:** Since 2022, the Court of Justice has started live-streaming its grand chamber hearings and the delivery of judgments on its website.
5. **Requesting a Hearing:** In direct actions (General Court), a party must request a hearing within a specific timeframe (usually 3 weeks after notice of the end of the written procedure).
6. **Interpretation:** Simultaneous interpretation is provided for all hearings in all 24 official EU languages.
7. **National Cause Lists:** The e-Justice Portal provides links to the national cause list systems of Member States (where available).
8. **Protocol:** The Court has strict rules on the conduct of hearings, including the "Report for the Hearing" which summarizes the facts and arguments.
9. **Remote Hearings:** Following the COVID-19 pandemic, the Court established technical and legal frameworks for conducting hearings via video conference in specific circumstances.
10. **Press Releases:** Significant hearings and all judgments are accompanied by press releases from the Court’s Communication Department to ensure transparency.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The oral part of the procedure... shall consist of the reading of the report... and of the hearing by the Court of agents, advisers and lawyers." — Article 59, Rules of Procedure of the Court of Justice.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
A solo advocate with a case before the CJEU must monitor the CURIA calendar religiously. If they wish to present oral arguments, they must proactively request a hearing and ensure they are familiar with the "Report for the Hearing" to focus their limited speaking time (often only 15-20 minutes) on the most critical points.
