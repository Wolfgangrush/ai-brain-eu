## Currency Warning
Arbitration is strictly excluded from the Brussels I-bis Regulation. Enforcement of arbitral awards is governed by the 1958 New York Convention, not EU Regulations.

## Key Facts
1. **Arbitration Exception:** Brussels I-bis does not apply to arbitration. This means EU rules on jurisdiction and recognition do not cover arbitral proceedings or the enforcement of awards.
2. **Mediation Directive:** Applies to cross-border civil/commercial disputes. Encourages the use of mediation before going to court.
3. **Enforceability of Settlements:** Member States must ensure that a written agreement resulting from mediation can be made enforceable by a court or notary (Art 6).
4. **Confidentiality:** Mediators are generally protected from being forced to give evidence in court regarding the mediation process.
5. **Limitation Periods:** Starting a mediation **suspends** the limitation period for the underlying claim (Art 8), ensuring parties don't lose their right to sue if mediation fails.
6. **Arb-Med:** Some EU states allow "Arb-Med" where an arbitrator acts as a mediator; if a settlement is reached, it can be recorded as a "consent award" for easier enforcement.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"This Regulation shall not apply to ... arbitration." - Article 1(2)(d), Brussels I-bis.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
- **Enforcement Strategy:** If you have a mediated settlement from another EU state, you must first get it certified as enforceable in its home state before you can use Brussels I-bis to enforce it elsewhere.
- **Tolling the Clock:** If a deadline is approaching, proposing mediation is a valid way to stop the clock on limitation periods.
- **Drafting Clauses:** Advocates should include "multi-tier" dispute resolution clauses (Mediation then Arbitration/Court) in commercial contracts.
