## Key Facts
1. **Jurisdictional Dualism:** Strict separation between the Judicial Order (private/criminal law) and the Administrative Order (public law).
2. **Judicial Order Hierarchy:** Tribunal Judiciaire (merged 1st instance), Cour d'Appel (2nd instance), and Cour de Cassation (Supreme Court for law only).
3. **Administrative Order Hierarchy:** Tribunal Administratif, Cour Administrative d'Appel, and Conseil d'État.
4. **Tribunal Judiciaire:** Created in 2020 by merging the *Tribunal de Grande Instance* and *Tribunal d'Instance*.
5. **Specialized Courts:** *Tribunal de Commerce* (lay business judges) and *Conseil de Prud'hommes* (labor disputes).
6. **Criminal Law:** Felonies (*crimes*) are tried by the *Cour d'Assises* with a jury. Misdemeanors (*délits*) by the *Tribunal Correctionnel*.
7. **Conseil Constitutionnel:** Conducts constitutional review, including the "Priority Preliminary Question" (QPC) mechanism.
8. **Tribunal des Conflits:** Resolves conflicts of jurisdiction between the two main orders.
9. **Avocats:** The legal profession is unified; advocates have a monopoly on pleading in most courts.
10. **Code Civil:** The Napoleonic Code remains the foundational text of French civil law.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The Court of Cassation does not rule on the merits of cases... it ensures that the law is applied in the same way throughout the territory." — Justice.gouv.fr.

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
The 2020 merger into the *Tribunal Judiciaire* simplified the 1st instance for solo practitioners. However, the dualism means that any case involving a public entity must be routed to the administrative courts, which have their own distinct procedural codes (CJA).
